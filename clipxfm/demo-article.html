The Quiet Revolution in How We Process Information at Work

As AI tools become woven into everyday routines, researchers are asking whether we are genuinely saving time — or simply shifting where the cognitive load lands.

By Margaret L. Forsythe  ·  Staff Writer May 14, 2025

Three years ago, a mid-size consulting firm in Chicago quietly ran an experiment. It gave half its analysts access to a suite of AI-assisted summarization tools; the other half continued working as before. The results, shared with this publication on the condition that the firm not be named, were striking — and not entirely in the way the executives had hoped.

Productivity, measured in tasks completed per day, rose by roughly 18 percent in the assisted group. But so did self-reported cognitive fatigue. "We assumed that offloading summarization would free up mental bandwidth," said one senior partner involved in the study. "Instead, people spent that bandwidth reviewing the AI's output, second-guessing it, and rewriting it. The work moved faster, but it didn't feel easier."

The Attention Economy Meets the Automation Economy

This tension sits at the heart of the current moment in workplace AI. Tools that once existed only in research laboratories — capable of condensing lengthy reports, translating documents in seconds, or flagging grammatical errors with near-human precision — are now standard features in email clients, word processors, and browser extensions used by millions of office workers daily.

"The bottleneck was never typing speed or reading speed. It was always judgment — knowing what matters and what doesn't. That part, so far, remains stubbornly human."

Dr. Anita Krishnamurthy, a cognitive scientist at the University of Edinburgh who studies human-computer interaction, argues that the tools are solving the wrong problem. "The bottleneck was never typing speed or reading speed," she told me over a video call last month. "It was always judgment — knowing what matters and what doesn't. That part, so far, remains stubbornly human."

Yet the tools keep improving, and adoption keeps accelerating. A report published last month by the Pew Research Center found that 43 percent of employed adults in the United States use some form of AI assistance at least once a week — up from 27 percent just eighteen months earlier. Among knowledge workers, the figure climbs to 61 percent.

When Convenience Becomes Infrastructure

Perhaps the more consequential shift is not in how AI is being used, but in how invisible it is becoming. Early adopters often spoke of AI tools as distinct, deliberate choices — something you switched on for a specific task. Increasingly, the tools are ambient: running in the background, available on demand, woven into the ordinary rhythm of reading, writing, and communicating.

This normalization raises questions that go beyond productivity metrics. If a portion of your written communication is quietly polished by an algorithm before it reaches the reader, whose voice is being heard? If a complex report is condensed by a model trained on millions of other documents, what subtle framings are being inherited — or lost?

For now, most users do not appear to be asking these questions. They are, by and large, satisfied. In survey after survey, the dominant sentiment is relief: relief at shorter inboxes, relief at faster first drafts, relief at not having to parse a forty-page PDF to find the three sentences that actually matter.

Whether that relief is earned, or merely deferred, remains to be seen.
